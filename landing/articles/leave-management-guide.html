Leave management is one of those things that feels simple until you're a company of 12 trying to run operations across two countries, each with their own public holiday calendars, religious observance requirements, and legal entitlements. What started as "just tell me when you're off" becomes a coordination problem with real legal and team-morale consequences.

This guide covers how to think about leave policy from first principles, what the regulations actually require in Indonesia and UAE, and how to build a system that scales without becoming a spreadsheet nightmare.

Why "just be flexible" isn't a leave policy

Many early-stage startups operate on informal trust: if you need a day off, take it. This works until it doesn't. Problems emerge when:

  • People don't take leave because there's no clear norm, leading to burnout
  • Someone takes two weeks during a critical sprint with no coverage plan
  • A team member resigns and asks to be paid out 30 days of accumulated leave you didn't know they had
  • A labour audit reveals you've been non-compliant with mandatory entitlements

A leave policy doesn't mean bureaucracy. It means clarity — both for the employee ("here's what you're entitled to") and for the company ("here's how we track it").

The legal baseline: Indonesia vs UAE

Both countries have non-negotiable minimums. Your policy can be more generous, but it cannot be less.

Designing your leave policy: five decisions to make

1. Accrual vs granted upfront

Do employees receive their full annual entitlement on day one of each year, or do they accrue it gradually (e.g., 1 day per month)? Most startups opt for granted upfront for simplicity, but accrual reduces risk if someone leaves early in the year having taken all their leave.

2. Carryover rules

Can unused leave roll over to the next year? Indonesian law requires that leave not taken within the year lapses (unless your policy extends it), but you must pay out any accumulated leave upon termination. UAE law requires payout of untaken leave on termination at whatever rate applies.

A clean approach: allow up to 5 days carryover with a 3-month expiry window in the new year. This encourages people to actually take their leave without creating unbounded liability.

3. Leave approval workflow

Who approves leave, and how far in advance must it be requested? Define minimums: "annual leave of 3+ consecutive days requires 2 weeks' notice; 1-2 days requires 48 hours." This isn't about control — it's about making coverage planning possible.

4. Leave during probation

Indonesian law: employees in the first 12 months haven't accrued annual leave entitlement yet. UAE law: employees in year 1 accrue at 2 days/month. Be explicit about what applies during probation — it's a frequent source of confusion and disputes.

5. Emergency and compassionate leave

Cover scenarios outside the standard types: bereavement (how many days, for which relatives), family emergencies, jury duty. A short, explicit policy here prevents awkward ad-hoc decisions when someone is already in a difficult moment.

The operational layer: making it work day-to-day

Policy without process is just words in a document. The operational requirements are:

  • A single source of truth for balances. Every employee should be able to see their current leave balance at any moment. No "check with HR" friction.
  • Automated public holiday calendars. Maintaining two countries' holiday calendars manually is error-prone. Use a system that handles this for you and reflects them automatically in leave calculations.
  • Manager visibility. The approving manager needs to see team availability before approving leave — not just a raw request with no context.
  • Leave without pay (LWP) tracking. For requests beyond entitlement, you need a clear audit trail of what was agreed and what the pay impact was.
  • Accrual reports at year-end. Before the calendar resets, you need a clear view of who has unused leave and what your carryover/payout liability looks like.
Multi-country tip: When running a team across Indonesia and UAE, be explicit about which country's rules apply to which employee. A UAE-based employee working for an Indonesian-registered entity creates ambiguity — usually the law of the country of employment applies. Always verify with local legal counsel.

When to formalise

The honest answer: earlier than feels necessary. At 5 people you can get away with informal norms. By 10, you have enough calendar complexity that overlapping leave starts causing real coordination problems. By 15 — especially across two countries — you absolutely need a written policy, a digital system, and consistent enforcement.

The cost of building this system is low. The cost of not having it — a labour dispute, a burned-out team member, a missed compliance requirement — is high.

Leave management isn't an HR admin task. It's a trust infrastructure. When your team knows exactly what they're entitled to, how to request it, and that their balance is accurate, you've removed a background stressor that affects morale and retention more than most founders realise.
